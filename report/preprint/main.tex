\begin{abstract}
We present a deep learning solver for optimal market-making in limit order books, combining the Deep BSDE method of Han, Jentzen, and E~(2018) with the McKean--Vlasov fictitious play framework of Han, Hu, and Long~(2022). Starting from the multi-dealer intensity control game of Cont and Xiong~(2024), we derive two computational formulations: (i)~a continuous inventory relaxation where the Poisson order flow is approximated by a diffusion, yielding a standard MV-FBSDE amenable to the Deep BSDE architecture; and (ii)~an exact jump-diffusion formulation (FBSDEJ) where the neural network outputs both the Brownian gradient $Z_t$ and the Poisson jump controls $U_t^\pm$. In both cases, optimal bid-ask quotes emerge directly from the learned gradient process via the HJB first-order condition, recovering the classical Avellaneda--Stoikov~(2008) spread $2/\alpha$ as a special case. We implement the solver in PyTorch with GPU support and conduct systematic stress tests under non-linear inventory penalties (quadratic, cubic, exponential), documenting the parameter threshold at which the backward driver loses Lipschitz continuity and the $Z_t$ gradient process explodes. To our knowledge, this is the first application of Deep MV-BSDE methods to limit order book market-making with explicit control recovery from the learned gradient object.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} backward stochastic differential equations, deep learning, mean-field games, limit order books, market-making, McKean--Vlasov

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

The interaction of algorithmic market makers in electronic markets poses a challenging stochastic control problem: each agent must optimise bid-ask quotes in continuous time, facing inventory risk, uncertain order flow, and strategic competition from other participants. Cont and Xiong~\cite{cont2024dynamics} formalise this as a stochastic differential game of intensity control among $N$ dealers, characterising Nash equilibria via a coupled system of Hamilton--Jacobi--Bellman (HJB) equations. Their model features Poisson order arrivals, discrete inventory increments, and execution probabilities that depend on competitors' quotes.

Separately, the Deep BSDE method introduced by E, Han, and Jentzen~\cite{e2017deep, han2018solving} has demonstrated that high-dimensional parabolic PDEs can be solved by reformulating them as backward stochastic differential equations (BSDEs) and approximating the gradient process $Z_t$ with neural networks. Han, Hu, and Long~\cite{han2022learning} extended this to McKean--Vlasov FBSDEs via a fictitious play outer loop, enabling the solution of mean-field games with general distribution dependence.

Despite the maturity of both literatures, no published work applies the Deep MV-BSDE framework directly to limit order book market-making. Cont and Xiong~\cite{cont2024dynamics} explicitly note that the Deep BSDE method could be used for best-response computation but employ direct numerical optimisation instead. Deep BSDE solvers for jump-diffusions exist~\cite{andersson2023deep} but have not been applied to market-making controls where the learned $Z_t$ and $U_t^\pm$ have direct financial interpretation as optimal quotes.

\paragraph{Contribution.} We bridge this gap by:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Deriving a continuous relaxation of the Cont--Xiong discrete inventory model that yields a 2D MV-FBSDE compatible with the standard Deep BSDE architecture, recovering the Avellaneda--Stoikov~\cite{avellaneda2008high} optimal spread as a special case.
  \item Implementing an exact jump-diffusion formulation (FBSDEJ) where the neural network outputs $(Z_t, U_t^+, U_t^-)$ and optimal quotes are extracted from the jump controls.
  \item Conducting systematic stress tests under non-linear inventory penalties that deliberately break the Lipschitz/monotonicity conditions required for convergence, documenting the precise parameter threshold of solver failure.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Mathematical Framework}
\label{sec:framework}

\subsection{The Cont--Xiong Dealer Market Model}

We consider $N$ market makers quoting around a mid-price $S_t = S_0 + \sigma W_t$ on a filtered probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{F}, \mathbb{P})$. Market maker $i$ quotes a centred ask spread $\delta_t^{a,i}$ and bid spread $\delta_t^{b,i}$, yielding ask and bid prices $S_t^{a,i} = S_t + \delta_t^{a,i}$ and $S_t^{b,i} = S_t - \delta_t^{b,i}$.

Order flow arrives as Poisson processes with intensities depending on quotes:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:poisson_intensity}
  \nu_t^{a,i} = \lambda^a f_a^i(\delta_t^{a,i}, \boldsymbol{\delta}_t^{-i}), \qquad
  \nu_t^{b,i} = \lambda^b f_b^i(\delta_t^{b,i}, \boldsymbol{\delta}_t^{-i}),
\end{equation}
where $\lambda^a, \lambda^b > 0$ are baseline arrival rates and $f_a^i, f_b^i$ are execution probability functions satisfying $\sum_{i=1}^N f_m^i \le 1$ for $m \in \{a,b\}$, following \cite[Assumption~2.1]{cont2024dynamics}.

Inventory evolves by discrete jumps of size $\Delta$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:inventory_discrete}
  \dd q_t^i = \Delta \bigl( \dd N_t^{b,i} - \dd N_t^{a,i} \bigr),
\end{equation}
where $N_t^{a,i}$ and $N_t^{b,i}$ are Poisson processes with intensities~\eqref{eq:poisson_intensity}. Each market maker maximises discounted expected profit:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:objective}
  J_i(\boldsymbol{\delta}^i, \boldsymbol{\delta}^{-i}; q_0^i) = \E\biggl[
    \int_0^\infty e^{-rt} \Bigl(
      \delta_t^{a,i} \Delta \, \dd N_t^{a,i} + \delta_t^{b,i} \Delta \, \dd N_t^{b,i}
      - \psi(q_t^i) \, \dd t
    \Bigr)
  \biggr],
\end{equation}
where $\psi: \R \to \R_+$ is an inventory holding penalty and $r > 0$ is a discount rate.

\subsection{Mean-Field Limit}
\label{sec:mean_field}

As $N \to \infty$, the empirical distribution of competitors' quotes converges to a deterministic measure $\mup$. The execution probability for a representative agent simplifies to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:exec_prob_mf}
  f(\delta, \mup) = \Lambda(\delta) \cdot h(\mup), \qquad \Lambda(\delta) = e^{-\alpha \delta},
\end{equation}
where $h(\mup)$ captures the competitive effect of the population's quoting distribution. The HJB equation for the value function $V(q)$ becomes
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hjb}
  rV(q) + \psi(q) - \lambda^a \sup_{\delta^a} \Bigl[ f_a(\delta^a, \mup) \Bigl( \delta^a \Delta + V(q - \Delta) - V(q) \Bigr) \Bigr]
  - \lambda^b \sup_{\delta^b} \Bigl[ f_b(\delta^b, \mup) \Bigl( \delta^b \Delta + V(q + \Delta) - V(q) \Bigr) \Bigr] = 0.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Continuous Relaxation (Option A)}
\label{sec:relaxation}

For compatibility with the Deep BSDE architecture, we approximate the discrete inventory process~\eqref{eq:inventory_discrete} with a continuous diffusion. In the regime $\Delta \to 0$, $\lambda^a, \lambda^b \to \infty$ with finite order flow, the Poisson process converges to a diffusion with matching mean and variance:
\begin{align}
  \dd S_t &= \sigma \, \dd W_t^S, \label{eq:forward_price} \\
  \dd q_t &= \bigl(\lambda^b f_b(\delta_t^b, \mup) - \lambda^a f_a(\delta_t^a, \mup)\bigr) \dd t
    + \sqrt{\lambda^b f_b(\delta_t^b, \mup) + \lambda^a f_a(\delta_t^a, \mup)} \, \dd W_t^q. \label{eq:forward_inventory}
\end{align}
The state $X_t = (S_t, q_t) \in \R^2$ is now a continuous diffusion, and the HJB~\eqref{eq:hjb} becomes a second-order PDE with finite differences replaced by derivatives. This recovers the continuous-inventory framework of Avellaneda and Stoikov~\cite{avellaneda2008high} in a mean-field setting.

\paragraph{Optimal quotes.} The first-order condition of the continuous HJB yields
\begin{equation}\label{eq:optimal_quotes}
  \delta_t^{a*} = \frac{1}{\alpha} + Z_t^q, \qquad
  \delta_t^{b*} = \frac{1}{\alpha} - Z_t^q,
\end{equation}
where $Z_t^q = \partial_q V(t, S_t, q_t)$ is the inventory gradient of the value function. At $q = 0$ (no inventory risk), the total spread is $\delta_t^{a*} + \delta_t^{b*} = 2/\alpha$, the classical Avellaneda--Stoikov result.

\subsection{Jump-Diffusion Formulation (Option B)}
\label{sec:jump}

Alternatively, we preserve the exact Poisson structure~\eqref{eq:inventory_discrete} and solve an FBSDE with jumps. The backward equation becomes
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fbsdej}
  \dd Y_t = -f(t, X_t, Y_t, Z_t, U_t^+, U_t^-) \, \dd t
    + Z_t \, \dd W_t^S
    + U_t^+ \, \dd \tilde{N}_t^b
    + U_t^- \, \dd \tilde{N}_t^a,
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{N}_t^m = N_t^m - \int_0^t \nu_s^m \, \dd s$ are compensated Poisson processes and:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $Z_t \in \R$: gradient of $V$ with respect to price (Brownian component),
  \item $U_t^+ = V(q + \Delta) - V(q)$: value change on buy execution,
  \item $U_t^- = V(q - \Delta) - V(q)$: value change on sell execution.
\end{itemize}
The neural network outputs the triple $(Z_t, U_t^+, U_t^-)$ at each time step.

\section{Numerical Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Deep BSDE Architecture}

We follow the Deep BSDE framework of Han et al.~\cite{han2018solving}. The time interval $[0, T]$ is discretised into $N_T$ steps with $\Delta t = T / N_T$. The BSDE~\eqref{eq:fbsdej} is approximated by the Euler--Maruyama scheme:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:euler}
  Y_{n+1} = Y_n - f(t_n, X_n, Y_n, Z_n) \Delta t + Z_n \cdot \Delta W_n,
\end{equation}
where each $Z_n$ is parameterised by a feedforward neural network (``subnet'') $\phi_n: \R^d \to \R^d$ with batch normalisation:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:subnet}
  Z_n = \frac{1}{d}\, \phi_n(X_{n+1}; \theta_n).
\end{equation}
The initial value $Y_0$ is a trainable scalar parameter. The loss function penalises the terminal mismatch:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loss}
  \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \E\bigl[ \ell\bigl( Y_{N_T} - g(X_T) \bigr) \bigr],
\end{equation}
where $\ell$ is a clipped Huber loss for numerical stability.

\paragraph{Architecture details.} Each subnet $\phi_n$ consists of two hidden layers with 32 neurons, batch normalisation (momentum 0.01), and ReLU activations. One independent subnet is used per time step (non-shared architecture). Training uses Adam with a piecewise constant learning rate schedule.

\subsection{Mean-Field Coupling via Fictitious Play}

Following Han et al.~\cite{han2022learning}, we solve the mean-field game iteratively:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Fix the population distribution $\mup$ (initialised to equilibrium).
  \item Solve the representative agent's BSDE given $\mup$ (inner loop: gradient descent on~\eqref{eq:loss}).
  \item Update $\mup$ from the empirical distribution of the trained agent's quotes.
  \item Repeat until convergence.
\end{enumerate}
In our implementation, the mean-field enters through the execution probability~\eqref{eq:exec_prob_mf}. The competitive factor $h(\mup)$ is approximated by a small neural network retrained every 100 gradient steps.

\subsection{Optimal Quote Extraction}

The key insight connecting the Deep BSDE output to the financial control is that the subnet output $Z_t$ directly provides the optimal quotes via~\eqref{eq:optimal_quotes}. No separate policy network is needed --- the gradient of the value function \emph{is} the control.

For the jump-diffusion formulation, each subnet outputs $(Z_t, U_t^+, U_t^-)$ and the optimal quotes are:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:optimal_quotes_jump}
  \delta_t^{a*} = \frac{1}{\alpha} - \frac{U_t^-}{\Delta}, \qquad
  \delta_t^{b*} = \frac{1}{\alpha} + \frac{U_t^+}{\Delta}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Spread Recovery Benchmark}

The solver recovers the Avellaneda--Stoikov optimal spread $2/\alpha = 1.333$ within 200 training iterations and maintains it stably through 5000 iterations. \Cref{fig:convergence} shows the training loss converging to $3.3 \times 10^{-5}$ and the value function $Y_0$ stabilising at $0.456$.

The finite-horizon theoretical value at $q = 0$ is
$$V_T(0) = \frac{2 \lambda e^{-1} / \alpha}{r} \bigl(1 - e^{-rT}\bigr) = 0.467,$$
confirming that the learned $Y_0 = 0.456$ is within 2.4\% of the analytical benchmark.

\subsection{Stress Testing Under Non-Linear Penalties}

We replace the standard quadratic penalty $\psi(q) = \phi q^2$ with increasingly non-linear alternatives and monitor the maximum gradient magnitude $\max_t |Z_t|$ during training.

The pattern in \Cref{tab:stress} shows that gradient magnitudes increase monotonically with penalty non-linearity. The exponential penalty drives $|Z_t|$ to values that destabilise the BSDE Euler scheme within hundreds of iterations, consistent with the violation of the Lipschitz condition on the backward driver $f$ required by convergence theory~\cite{han2022learning}.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have demonstrated that the Deep Mean-Field BSDE framework can be successfully applied to optimal market-making in limit order books, a setting not previously explored in the deep BSDE literature. The continuous relaxation of the Cont--Xiong model yields a tractable 2D MV-FBSDE whose solution recovers the Avellaneda--Stoikov optimal spread, while the jump-diffusion formulation preserves the exact microstructure dynamics at the cost of higher training variance.

The systematic stress tests under non-linear penalties provide empirical evidence for the practical limitations of the Deep BSDE method: as inventory penalties become increasingly non-linear, the backward driver's Lipschitz constant grows, the gradient process $Z_t$ explodes, and the solver fails to converge. Characterising the precise failure threshold constitutes a diagnostic contribution complementary to existing convergence theory.

\paragraph{Future work.} Natural extensions include: (i)~common noise in the mean-field coupling, requiring conditional McKean--Vlasov dynamics and potentially rough path signatures to encode the shared shock history; (ii)~comparison of Nash equilibrium and Pareto optimal solutions to quantify the price of anarchy in market-making; and (iii)~scaling to higher-dimensional state spaces with multiple assets.
