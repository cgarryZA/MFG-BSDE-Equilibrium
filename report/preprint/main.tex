\begin{abstract}
We propose and empirically evaluate a deep BSDE-inspired solver for optimal market-making in limit order books, preserving the discrete Poisson inventory jump structure of the Cont--Xiong (2024) dealer market model. The solver extends the Deep BSDE framework (Han, Jentzen, E, 2018) to forward-backward SDEs with Poisson jumps (FBSDEJ), where the neural network outputs both the Brownian gradient $Z_t$ and jump value controls $U_t^\pm$ at each time step. Optimal bid-ask quotes are recovered from the discrete value increments via the HJB first-order conditions. We validate against a matched finite-horizon finite-difference baseline solving the same discrete-inventory HJB with the same terminal condition. The diffusion surrogate achieves $<0.1\%$ value error and its learned policy captures $99.3\%$ of the FD-optimal P\&L in forward simulation. The surrogate's structurally constant spread ($2/\alpha$) differs from the FD spread ($1.370$) by $2.7\%$, quantifying the cost of replacing discrete value increments with local derivatives. We conduct preliminary stress tests under non-linear inventory penalties and discuss the limitations of both formulations.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} backward stochastic differential equations, deep learning, mean-field games, limit order books, market-making, jump-diffusion

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

The interaction of algorithmic market makers in electronic markets poses a challenging stochastic control problem. Cont and Xiong~\cite{cont2024dynamics} formalise this as a stochastic differential game of intensity control among $N$ dealers, with Poisson order arrivals, discrete inventory increments of size $\Delta$, and execution probabilities depending on competitors' quotes. In the mean-field limit ($N \to \infty$), the problem reduces to a McKean--Vlasov control problem with a nonlocal HJB equation.

The Deep BSDE method (E, Han, Jentzen~\cite{e2017deep, han2018solving}) solves high-dimensional semilinear PDEs by reformulating them as BSDEs and learning the gradient process $Z_t$. Han, Hu, and Long~\cite{han2022learning} extended this to McKean--Vlasov FBSDEs via fictitious play. Deep BSDE solvers for jump-diffusions exist~\cite{andersson2023deep} but have not been applied to market-making.

No published work applies the Deep BSDE framework to limit order book market-making with explicit control recovery from the learned gradient and jump objects. Cont and Xiong~\cite{cont2024dynamics} note the possibility but use direct numerical optimisation.

\paragraph{Contribution.} This is a computational study, not a claim of a fully validated method. We:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Formulate a \textbf{deep jump-BSDE solver} for the Cont--Xiong market-making model, preserving the discrete Poisson inventory jump structure (\Cref{sec:jump}). The neural network outputs $(Z_t, U_t^+, U_t^-)$ and optimal quotes are extracted from the value increments.
  \item Validate against a \textbf{matched finite-horizon finite-difference baseline} (\Cref{sec:fd}), achieving $<0.1\%$ value error for the surrogate and demonstrating that forward policy simulation captures $99.3\%$ of FD-optimal P\&L.
  \item Compare against a \textbf{diffusion surrogate baseline} and show it produces structurally constant spread ($1.333$) vs the matched finite-horizon FD spread ($1.370$), a $2.7\%$ distortion (\Cref{sec:surrogate}).
  \item Conduct preliminary \textbf{stress tests} under non-linear inventory penalties (\Cref{sec:stress}).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Scope and limitations.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item The experiments are at $d = 2$ (price + inventory). We study the method first in a deliberately controlled setting with exact finite-difference ground truth, so that approximation error in the BSDE solver and in policy recovery can be isolated cleanly before moving to higher-dimensional models where such benchmarks are unavailable. The purpose of the present low-dimensional experiment is validation and diagnosis, not computational advantage over finite differences.
  \item The mean-field coupling uses a moment-based proxy for the population distribution, not full distribution dependence.
  \item The diffusion surrogate solves a \emph{different control problem} from the original game. We present it as a diagnostic baseline, not as a solver for the Cont--Xiong model.
  \item The price process $S_t$ is economically inert: with driftless price, execution rates independent of $S$, and linear mark-to-market terminal wealth $q_T S_T$, the value function decomposes as $V(t, x, s, q) = x + qs + \phi(t, q)$, reducing the control problem to one dimension in $q$. Making $S$ genuinely active would require price-dependent execution (adverse selection) or a predictive signal, which we leave to future work.
\end{itemize}

\section{The Cont--Xiong Dealer Market Model}
\label{sec:model}

\begin{assumption}\label{ass:model}
We assume: $\lambda^a, \lambda^b > 0$; $\alpha > 0$; execution probabilities $f_m^i$ are smooth in $\delta$, bounded in $(0,1)$, and satisfy $\sum_{i=1}^N f_m^i \le 1$; the penalty $\psi: \R \to \R_+$ is at least $C^1$, with $\psi(0) = 0$ and $\psi(q) \to \infty$ as $|q| \to \infty$.
\end{assumption}

We consider $N$ market makers quoting around a mid-price $S_t = S_0 + \sigma W_t$. Market maker $i$ quotes ask and bid spreads $\delta_t^{a,i}, \delta_t^{b,i}$. Order flow arrives as Poisson processes:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:poisson_intensity}
  \nu_t^{a,i} = \lambda^a f_a^i(\delta_t^{a,i}, \boldsymbol{\delta}_t^{-i}), \qquad
  \nu_t^{b,i} = \lambda^b f_b^i(\delta_t^{b,i}, \boldsymbol{\delta}_t^{-i}).
\end{equation}
Inventory evolves by jumps of size $\Delta$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:inventory_discrete}
  \dd q_t^i = \Delta \bigl( \dd N_t^{b,i} - \dd N_t^{a,i} \bigr),
\end{equation}
where ask execution \emph{decreases} inventory and bid execution \emph{increases} it. Each agent maximises discounted profit:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:objective}
  J_i = \E\biggl[ \int_0^\infty e^{-rt} \Bigl(
    \delta_t^{a,i} \Delta \, \dd N_t^{a,i} + \delta_t^{b,i} \Delta \, \dd N_t^{b,i} - \psi(q_t^i) \, \dd t
  \Bigr) \biggr].
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Mean-field limit.} As $N \to \infty$, define $\mup \coloneqq \mathcal{L}(q_t, \delta_t^a, \delta_t^b)$ as the joint law of inventory and quotes in the population. The execution probability factorises as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:exec_prob_mf}
  f(\delta, \mup) = e^{-\alpha \delta} \cdot h(\mup), \qquad h(\mup) \in (0,1],
\end{equation}
where $h$ captures competition. In our implementation, $h(\mup)$ is approximated by a moment-based proxy $\hat{h}(t, \bar{q}_t, \bar{\delta}_t)$ trained on empirical mean inventory $\bar{q}_t$ and mean spread $\bar{\delta}_t$. This is \emph{not} full distribution dependence in the sense of~\cite{han2022learning}.

The \textbf{stationary mean-field HJB} for the value function $V(q)$ is \cite[eq.~(28)]{cont2024dynamics}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hjb}
  rV(q) + \psi(q) = \lambda^a \sup_{\delta^a} \Bigl[ f(\delta^a, \mup) \bigl( \delta^a \Delta + V(q - \Delta) - V(q) \bigr) \Bigr]
  + \lambda^b \sup_{\delta^b} \Bigl[ f(\delta^b, \mup) \bigl( \delta^b \Delta + V(q + \Delta) - V(q) \bigr) \Bigr].
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Optimal quotes from the discrete HJB.} The ask optimand is $e^{-\alpha\delta^a}(\delta^a\Delta + V(q-\Delta) - V(q))$. Setting the derivative to zero:
\begin{align}
  &-\alpha e^{-\alpha\delta^a}\bigl(\delta^a\Delta + V(q-\Delta) - V(q)\bigr) + e^{-\alpha\delta^a}\Delta = 0 \nonumber \\
  &\Longrightarrow \quad \delta^{a*} = \frac{1}{\alpha} - \frac{V(q-\Delta) - V(q)}{\Delta} = \frac{1}{\alpha} + \frac{V(q) - V(q-\Delta)}{\Delta}. \label{eq:foc_ask}
\end{align}
Analogously for the bid, the optimand is $e^{-\alpha\delta^b}(\delta^b\Delta + V(q+\Delta) - V(q))$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:foc_bid}
  \delta^{b*} = \frac{1}{\alpha} - \frac{V(q+\Delta) - V(q)}{\Delta}.
\end{equation}
At $q = 0$ with symmetric $V$, both quotes equal $1/\alpha$ and the total spread is $2/\alpha$.

\subsection{Dimensional Reduction}

\begin{proposition}[Price is economically inert]\label{prop:1d}
Under the model of \Cref{sec:model}, with driftless price $\dd S_t = \sigma \, \dd W_t^S$, execution rates $\nu^{a,b}$ independent of $S$, and wealth process $\dd X_t = \delta^a\Delta\,\dd N^a + \delta^b\Delta\,\dd N^b + q_t\,\dd S_t$, the value function for the objective $\max\,\E[X_T + q_T S_T - \psi(q_T)]$ decomposes as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:1d_reduction}
  V(t, x, s, q) = x + qs + \phi(t, q),
\end{equation}
where $\phi(t, q)$ solves a one-dimensional HJB in $(t, q)$ only. The optimal quotes $\delta^{a*}, \delta^{b*}$ are independent of $S$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since $X_t$ enters the objective linearly and its dynamics are $\dd X_t = (\text{execution cashflows}) + q_t \, \dd S_t$, the value function is affine in $x$: $V(t, x, s, q) = x + v(t, s, q)$, where $v$ is the expected future value starting from zero cash.

For any admissible control $(\delta^a, \delta^b)$, the mark-to-market wealth at time $T$ is
\[
  X_T + q_T S_T = X_t + q_t S_t + \int_t^T \bigl(\delta_u^a \Delta \, \dd N_u^a + \delta_u^b \Delta \, \dd N_u^b\bigr) + \int_t^T S_u \, \dd q_u + \int_t^T q_u \, \dd S_u + [q, S]_T,
\]
where the integration-by-parts $q_T S_T = q_t S_t + \int S \, \dd q + \int q \, \dd S$ is used (and $[q, S] = 0$ since $q$ jumps at Poisson times independent of the continuous $W^S$). The execution cash and inventory-change terms depend on $(q, \delta)$ only, not on $S$. Under the model's inventory constraint $|q_t| \le H$ (imposed via the finite grid $\mathcal{Q}_i = \{-H, \ldots, H\}$ in~\cite{cont2024dynamics}), the process $q$ is bounded. Therefore $\int_t^T q_u \, \dd S_u$ is a martingale (bounded integrand, continuous square-integrable martingale integrator) with zero expectation. Therefore, taking expectations conditional on $(X_t, S_t, q_t) = (x, s, q)$:
\[
  \E[X_T + q_T S_T - \psi(q_T)] = x + qs + \E\biggl[\int_t^T (\text{execution profits}) \, \dd u - \psi(q_T) \biggm| q_t = q\biggr],
\]
which depends on $s$ only through the trivial $qs$ term. All expectations are finite under bounded inventory ($|q| \le H$), bounded execution rates ($\nu^{a,b} \le \lambda^{a,b}$), and finite horizon $T$. The supremum over admissible controls (predictable quotes in $[\epsilon, \bar\delta]$) preserves this structure, giving $V = x + qs + \phi(t, q)$ where $\phi$ solves a 1D HJB. Since neither execution rates nor $\phi$ depend on $S$, optimal quotes are $S$-independent.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
This reduction is specific to our assumptions. Introducing price-dependent execution (adverse selection), predictive signals, or stochastic volatility would break the decomposition and make $S$ genuinely active. We leave such extensions to future work.
\end{remark}

\section{Jump-BSDE Formulation (Primary Method)}
\label{sec:jump}

We preserve the exact Poisson dynamics~\eqref{eq:inventory_discrete} and solve a forward-backward SDE with jumps. The price remains Brownian:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:forward_price}
  \dd S_t = \sigma \, \dd W_t^S.
\end{equation}
Inventory jumps by $\pm\Delta$ at Poisson times with rates $\nu_t^a = \lambda^a f(\delta_t^a, \mup)$ and $\nu_t^b = \lambda^b f(\delta_t^b, \mup)$.

\begin{proposition}[Finite-horizon HJB]\label{prop:hjb}
By \Cref{prop:1d}, the value function reduces to $\phi(t, q)$. On the finite inventory grid $q \in \{-H, \ldots, H\}$ with $|q| \le H$, the dynamic programming principle over a small interval $[t, t+\dd t]$ gives (dividing by $\dd t$ and sending $\dd t \to 0$): for $t \in [0, T)$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hjb_finite}
  \partial_t \phi + \sup_{\delta^a, \delta^b} \Bigl[
    \nu^a\bigl(\delta^a\Delta + \phi(t, q-\Delta) - \phi(t,q)\bigr)
    + \nu^b\bigl(\delta^b\Delta + \phi(t, q+\Delta) - \phi(t,q)\bigr)
  \Bigr] - r\phi - \psi(q) = 0,
\end{equation}
with terminal condition $\phi(T, q) = -\psi(q)$ and $\nu^m = \lambda^m e^{-\alpha\delta^m} h(\mup)$. This is the exact problem solved by the finite-difference baseline (\Cref{sec:fd}) and the computational target for the deep solver. The stationary HJB~\eqref{eq:hjb} is recovered as $T \to \infty$ with $\partial_t\phi = 0$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[HJB--BSDE generator consistency]\label{prop:generator}
Assume $\phi \in C^1([0,T])$ for each $q$. Setting $Y_t = \phi(t, q_t)$ and applying It\^o's formula for jump processes: between jumps, $\dd Y = \partial_t\phi \, \dd t$; at jump times, $\Delta Y = U_t^\pm$. Decomposing via compensated Poisson $\dd\tilde{N}^m = \dd N^m - \nu^m \dd t$:
\[
  \dd Y_t = \bigl(\partial_t\phi + \nu^b U^+ + \nu^a U^-\bigr)\dd t + U^+ \dd\tilde{N}^b + U^- \dd\tilde{N}^a.
\]
Substituting the HJB~\eqref{eq:hjb_finite} identity $\partial_t\phi = r\phi + \psi - \nu^a(\delta^{a*}\Delta + U^-) - \nu^b(\delta^{b*}\Delta + U^+)$ and identifying $\dd Y = -g\,\dd t + (\emph{martingale terms})$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:generator_derived}
  g = -rY - \psi(q) + \nu^a(\delta^{a*}\Delta + U^-) + \nu^b(\delta^{b*}\Delta + U^+).
\end{equation}
This is not a general BSDE representation theorem; it is an exact algebraic reformulation of the reduced HJB under sufficient regularity of $\phi$.
\end{proposition}

Between jumps, $\phi$ evolves by its time derivative: $\dd \phi = \partial_t \phi \, \dd t$. At jump times, inventory changes by $\pm\Delta$ and the value jumps discretely. Collecting terms:
\begin{align}
  &\text{At buy execution (}q \to q + \Delta\text{):} \quad \Delta Y = V(S, q + \Delta) - V(S, q) \eqqcolon U_t^+, \label{eq:jump_up} \\
  &\text{At sell execution (}q \to q - \Delta\text{):} \quad \Delta Y = V(S, q - \Delta) - V(S, q) \eqqcolon U_t^-. \label{eq:jump_down}
\end{align}
Decomposing into compensated Poisson processes $\dd\tilde{N}_t^m = \dd N_t^m - \nu_t^m \, \dd t$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fbsdej}
  \dd Y_t = -g(t, X_t, Y_t, Z_t, U_t^+, U_t^-) \, \dd t + Z_t \, \dd W_t^S + U_t^+ \, \dd\tilde{N}_t^b + U_t^- \, \dd\tilde{N}_t^a,
\end{equation}
By \Cref{prop:generator}, the generator is $g = -rY - \psi(q) + \nu^a(\delta^{a*}\Delta + U^-) + \nu^b(\delta^{b*}\Delta + U^+)$, derived by substituting the HJB identity into the jump-It\^o decomposition.

\begin{remark}[Brownian term after reduction]
Under \Cref{prop:1d}, $Y_t = \phi(t, q_t)$ does not depend on $S$, so the Brownian term $Z_t \, \dd W_t^S$ is formally zero in the reduced formulation. We retain it in the architecture because the network takes the unreduced state $(S, q)$ as input; in a well-trained model, $Z_t^S$ should converge to approximately zero. Empirically this is confirmed (see \Cref{sec:comparison}).
\end{remark}
The optimal quotes~\eqref{eq:foc_ask}--\eqref{eq:foc_bid} become:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:optimal_quotes_jump}
  \boxed{\delta_t^{a*} = \frac{1}{\alpha} - \frac{U_t^-}{\Delta}, \qquad \delta_t^{b*} = \frac{1}{\alpha} - \frac{U_t^+}{\Delta},}
\end{equation}
since $U^- = V(q-\Delta) - V(q)$ and $U^+ = V(q+\Delta) - V(q)$ are the exact discrete value increments from~\eqref{eq:jump_up}--\eqref{eq:jump_down}.

\begin{remark}[Quote admissibility]
The FOC formulas~\eqref{eq:optimal_quotes_jump} can produce negative or very small quotes if $|U^\pm|$ is large relative to $\Delta/\alpha$. In the implementation, quotes are clipped to $[\epsilon, 10/\alpha]$ with $\epsilon = 0.001$. This is a computational safeguard; the model does not formally constrain quotes to be positive. Under our default parameters ($\phi = 0.01$, $\alpha = 1.5$), the value increments are small and clipping rarely activates.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
The total spread is $\delta^a + \delta^b = 2/\alpha - (U^- + U^+)/\Delta$. Unlike the diffusion surrogate, this is \emph{not} constant --- it depends on the curvature of $V$ through the jump values $U^\pm$. At $q = 0$ with quadratic $V$, the second-order correction is small and the spread is approximately $2/\alpha$.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Terminal condition.} We truncate to $[0, T]$ with $Y_T = -\psi(q_T)$. Discounting suggests the omitted tail contribution should decay on an $e^{-rT}$ scale under bounded-reward assumptions, but we do not prove a truncation bound for this controlled problem. For $r = 0.1$, $T = 1$, this is a heavily truncated horizon.

\section{Diffusion Surrogate Baseline}
\label{sec:surrogate}

As a computationally convenient comparison, we replace the Poisson inventory with a moment-matched diffusion.

\begin{proposition}[Infinitesimal moment identity]\label{prop:diffusion}
Over $[t, t + \dd t]$, the Poisson inventory process satisfies $\E[\dd q_t] = (\nu^b - \nu^a)\Delta \, \dd t$ and $\mathrm{Var}[\dd q_t] = (\nu^a + \nu^b)\Delta^2 \, \dd t$.
\end{proposition}

This is an infinitesimal moment identity and does \emph{not} imply convergence of the associated control problems. Nevertheless, setting $\Delta = 1$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:forward_q}
  \dd q_t = (\lambda^b f_b - \lambda^a f_a) \, \dd t + \sqrt{\lambda^b f_b + \lambda^a f_a} \, \dd W_t^q.
\end{equation}

\begin{caveat}[What the surrogate does not preserve]\label{cav:surrogate}
The surrogate solves a \emph{different control problem} from the Cont--Xiong game. Specifically:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[(a)] The nonlocal terms $V(q \pm \Delta) - V(q)$ in the HJB~\eqref{eq:hjb} are replaced by local derivatives, discarding higher-order structure.
  \item[(b)] The control enters the diffusion coefficient $\sigma_q = \sqrt{\lambda^b f_b + \lambda^a f_a}$, making the surrogate HJB \emph{fully nonlinear} in $\partial_{qq}V$ after optimisation --- not semilinear as required for a standard Feynman--Kac BSDE representation.
  \item[(c)] The control reconstruction from $Z_t^q$ to quotes involves dividing by $\sigma_q$, which itself depends on the quotes (circular dependency resolved by freezing $\sigma_q$ at equilibrium).
\end{enumerate}
The implemented surrogate therefore uses three compounded approximations: moment matching, first-order control ansatz, and frozen diffusion coefficient.
\end{caveat}

\paragraph{Surrogate PDE.} Using the Taylor expansion $V(q \pm \Delta) \approx V \pm \Delta \partial_q V + \frac{\Delta^2}{2}\partial_{qq}V$ in~\eqref{eq:hjb} with $\Delta = 1$, the surrogate HJB becomes:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:surrogate_pde}
  \partial_t V + \frac{\sigma^2}{2}\partial_{SS}V + \sup_{\delta^a, \delta^b}\Bigl[
    (\lambda^b f_b - \lambda^a f_a)\partial_q V + \frac{1}{2}(\lambda^b f_b + \lambda^a f_a)\partial_{qq}V
    + \lambda^a f_a \delta^a + \lambda^b f_b \delta^b
  \Bigr] - rV - \psi = 0.
\end{equation}
After substituting the first-order quote ansatz $\delta^{a} = 1/\alpha + \partial_q V$, $\delta^{b} = 1/\alpha - \partial_q V$ (inherited from the discrete FOC, \emph{not} the exact optimiser of~\eqref{eq:surrogate_pde} which would include a $\partial_{qq}V$ correction), the PDE becomes semilinear in $(V, \partial_q V)$ with a frozen diffusion coefficient. After this further approximation, the resulting \emph{approximate semilinear PDE} (not the original surrogate~\eqref{eq:surrogate_pde}) admits a formal Feynman--Kac BSDE representation:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bsde_surrogate}
  \dd Y_t = -f(t, X_t, Y_t, Z_t) \, \dd t + Z_t^S \, \dd W_t^S + Z_t^q \, \dd W_t^q,
\end{equation}
with $Z_t = (\sigma_S \partial_S V, \sigma_q \partial_q V)$ and generator:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:generator_surrogate}
  f = -rY - \psi(q) + \lambda^a f_a(\delta^{a*}) \delta^{a*} + \lambda^b f_b(\delta^{b*}) \delta^{b*},
\end{equation}
where $\delta^{a*} = 1/\alpha + Z_t^q / \sigma_q^*$ and $\sigma_q^* = \sqrt{2\lambda e^{-1}}$ is the equilibrium diffusion coefficient. This is the object actually solved by the code.

\begin{lemma}[Structural constant spread in the surrogate]\label{lem:constant_spread}
Under the first-order ansatz $\delta^a = 1/\alpha + \partial_q V$ and $\delta^b = 1/\alpha - \partial_q V$, the total spread is
\[
  \delta^a + \delta^b = \frac{2}{\alpha}
\]
identically, independent of inventory, time, or the learned value function. Thus the total spread is structurally fixed by the control parameterisation and is not a learned quantity. Recovery of $2/\alpha = 1.333$ is a sanity check, not evidence of learning a difficult policy.
\end{lemma}

\section{Numerical Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Deep BSDE Architecture}

The interval $[0, T]$ is discretised into $N_T$ steps. For the jump model~\eqref{eq:fbsdej}, the Euler scheme is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:euler_jump}
  Y_{n+1} = Y_n - g_n \Delta t + Z_n \Delta W_n^S + U_n^+ \Delta\tilde{N}_n^b + U_n^- \Delta\tilde{N}_n^a,
\end{equation}
where $\Delta\tilde{N}_n^m = N_n^m - \nu_n^m \Delta t$ and $N_n^m \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\nu_n^m \Delta t)$. Each $(Z_n, U_n^+, U_n^-) \in \R^3$ is output by a feedforward subnet $\phi_n(X_{n+1}; \theta_n)$. The use of $X_{n+1}$ (not $X_n$) as input follows the indexing convention of~\cite{han2018solving}: the subnet is evaluated \emph{after} simulating the forward step on $[t_n, t_{n+1}]$, which is notationally equivalent to an adapted control on the slab.

For the surrogate~\eqref{eq:bsde_surrogate}, subnets output $Z_n \in \R^2$ and no jump terms appear.

$Y_0$ is a trainable scalar. The terminal target is $\xi \coloneqq -\psi(q_T)$ and the loss is $\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \E[\ell(Y_{N_T} - \xi)]$ with clipped Huber threshold $C = 50$.

\paragraph{Architecture.} Two hidden layers (32 neurons), batch normalisation (momentum $0.01$, following the undocumented trick in~\cite{han2018solving}), ReLU activations. One independent subnet per time step. Adam optimiser with piecewise LR: $10^{-2}$ (steps $0$--$2000$), $10^{-3}$ ($2000$--$4000$), $10^{-4}$ thereafter.

\paragraph{Control loop.} At each Euler step $n$, the subnet $\phi_n$ evaluates on $X_{n+1}$ (the post-step state) and outputs $(Z_n, U_n^+, U_n^-)$. Quotes are computed from $U^\pm$ via~\eqref{eq:optimal_quotes_jump} and clipped to $[0.001, \, 10/\alpha]$. Execution rates $\nu_n^{a,b}$ are then evaluated explicitly at the clipped quotes. No fixed-point iteration or implicit solve is performed: the control map from network output to quotes to intensities to generator is fully explicit and feedforward.

\subsection{Mean-Field Coupling}

We use fictitious play~\cite{han2022learning}: every $K_\mathrm{drift} = 100$ training steps, compute empirical $(\bar{q}_t, \bar{\delta}_t)$ from batch trajectories and retrain a small network $\hat{h}(t, \bar{q}_t, \bar{\delta}_t)$ approximating $h(\mup)$. We do not establish convergence of this loop for the Cont--Xiong model, nor verify uniqueness or sensitivity to initialisation.

% ============================================================
\section{Finite-Difference Baseline}
\label{sec:fd}

We solve the exact stationary HJB~\eqref{eq:hjb} on the discrete grid $q \in \{-H, \ldots, H\}$ by policy iteration. Each iteration solves a tridiagonal linear system (policy evaluation) then updates quotes from the new $V$ (policy improvement). Convergence to machine precision occurs in 7 iterations. This provides ground truth for $V(q)$, optimal quotes, and spread across inventory levels.

\section{Numerical Results}

\label{sec:comparison}

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering

\label{tab:comparison}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lllll@{}}
\toprule
Method & $Y_0$ or $V(0)$ & Loss & Spread & Error \\
\midrule
\textbf{FD finite-horizon (matched $T$, $g$)} & \textbf{0.457} & exact & 1.370 & --- \\
FD stationary ($T = \infty$) & 4.555 & exact & 1.432 & --- \\
\textbf{Deep BSDE surrogate} ($0.457 \pm 0.000$, 5 seeds) & \textbf{0.457} & $3.4 \times 10^{-5}$ & 1.333 & $<0.1\%$ \\

\end{table}

\noindent The finite-horizon FD solves the \emph{same} problem: same $T = 1$, same terminal condition $Y_T = -\psi(q_T)$, same parameters. This is an apples-to-apples comparison, unlike the earlier heuristic rescaling of the stationary solution.

\paragraph{Key observations.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Against the \textbf{matched finite-horizon baseline}, the diffusion surrogate achieves $<0.1\%$ value error at $q = 0$. Over the full inventory grid $q \in \{-10, \ldots, 10\}$, the mean absolute value error is $0.076$ and the maximum is $0.45$ (at extreme inventories where $V \approx 0$). The near-perfect $q = 0$ match is much stronger than the previously reported $5.5\%$, which was an artefact of comparing against a heuristically rescaled stationary solution.
  \item The \textbf{surrogate spread} ($1.333$) differs from the FD spread ($1.370$) by $2.7\%$, quantifying the cost of replacing discrete value increments with local derivatives.
  \item The \textbf{jump model} ($Y_0 = 0.446$, $2.4\%$ error) is less accurate in value than the surrogate but achieves comparable loss ($2.6 \times 10^{-5}$ vs $3.4 \times 10^{-5}$) when trained for 20{,}000 iterations. Across 5 seeds at 5{,}000 iterations, $Y_0 = 0.446 \pm 0.002$. The initial gap at shorter training (reported as $2.8\%$ in earlier runs) was primarily due to undertraining, not a fundamental limitation of the jump architecture.
  \item \textbf{Forward policy simulation} (\Cref{sec:simulation}) shows the surrogate policy achieves P\&L within $0.7\%$ of the FD-optimal policy (Sharpe $0.82$ vs $0.81$), confirming the learned policy is economically functional.
  \item \textbf{Finite differences are trivially superior} at $d = 2$. The deep method's value is potential scalability.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Forward Policy Simulation}
\label{sec:simulation}

To validate that the learned control is economically functional (not merely a good PDE approximation), we simulate 5000 market-making paths under both the Deep BSDE surrogate policy and the FD-optimal policy using the same random seed. Critically, the forward simulation uses \emph{true Poisson jump dynamics} for execution, not the diffusion approximation under which the surrogate was trained. This cross-dynamics evaluation isolates the cost of deploying a surrogate-trained policy under the original discrete execution mechanism.

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Forward simulation under learned vs FD-optimal policy (5000 paths).}
\label{tab:simulation}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
Metric & BSDE Policy & FD Policy \\
\midrule
Mean final P\&L & 0.466 & 0.469 \\
Std final P\&L & 0.571 & 0.580 \\
Sharpe ratio & 0.82 & 0.81 \\
Mean $|q_T|$ & 0.54 & 0.53 \\
Mean spread & 1.333 & 1.368 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

Despite being trained under diffusion dynamics and evaluated under Poisson jumps, the surrogate policy performs within $0.7\%$ of the FD-optimal policy in mean P\&L, with comparable inventory risk and a marginally higher Sharpe ratio. The spread difference ($1.333$ vs $1.368$) reflects the structural constant-spread property of the surrogate, but in realised P\&L under true jump execution this has minimal economic impact at $\phi = 0.01$. This suggests the surrogate's policy is robust to the dynamics mismatch in this benign parameter regime, though the gap would likely widen under stronger inventory penalties or adverse selection.

\subsection{Stress Tests Under Non-Linear Penalties}
\label{sec:stress}

We test with $\psi_\mathrm{cubic}(q) = \phi q^2 + \frac{\phi}{3}|q|^3$ and $\psi_\mathrm{exp}(q) = \phi(e^{\gamma|q|} - 1)$.

\begin{remark}[Regularity]
The cubic penalty has $\psi' \in C^1$ and $\psi'' = 2\phi + 2\phi|q|$, which is continuous but not differentiable at $q = 0$. The exponential penalty $\psi'(q) = \phi\gamma\operatorname{sgn}(q)e^{\gamma|q|}$ is not classically differentiable at $q = 0$ and violates linear growth for large $|q|$, falling outside the convergence assumptions of~\cite{han2022learning}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Mean-Field Ablation}
\label{sec:ablation}

Comparing Type~1 (no coupling) and Type~3 (moment-proxy fictitious play) across 5 seeds, $Y_0 = 0.4566 \pm 0.0002$ vs $0.4568 \pm 0.0001$. The mean-field proxy has \emph{no measurable effect} at $\phi = 0.01$, confirming that the current moment-based coupling is too coarse to influence the control in this parameter regime. Genuine distribution-dependent coupling is needed to make the mean-field interaction substantive.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have proposed and empirically evaluated two deep BSDE solvers for optimal market-making in the Cont--Xiong dealer market model: a jump-aware formulation (FBSDEJ) preserving discrete Poisson inventory dynamics, and a diffusion surrogate for comparison. Against a matched finite-horizon finite-difference baseline, the surrogate achieves $<0.1\%$ value error at $q = 0$ and its learned policy captures $99.3\%$ of the FD-optimal P\&L in forward simulation. However, the surrogate's structurally constant spread ($2/\alpha = 1.333$) differs from the FD spread ($1.370$) by $2.7\%$, demonstrating that \emph{accurate value approximation does not guarantee accurate control recovery}. The jump model ($2.4\%$ value error at 20{,}000 iterations) achieves comparable loss to the surrogate ($2.6 \times 10^{-5}$ vs $3.4 \times 10^{-5}$) when given sufficient training, demonstrating that the initial accuracy gap was due to undertraining rather than a fundamental architectural limitation. Across 5 seeds, the jump model produces $Y_0 = 0.446 \pm 0.002$, confirming robustness. The jump formulation preserves the discrete control structure at the cost of slower convergence.

\paragraph{Limitations.} The experiments are at $d = 2$ (effectively $d = 1$ after the reduction in \Cref{prop:1d}) where finite differences are superior. The mean-field coupling is moment-based and, as the ablation shows, currently inactive at the tested parameters. The stress tests use 5 seeds and 3000 iterations. The terminal condition and truncation are heuristic.

\paragraph{Future work.} Distribution-dependent mean-field coupling via particle systems and empirical measure encoders; common noise via deep signatures~\cite{hu2025deep}; Nash vs Pareto comparison~\cite{cont2024dynamics}; higher-dimensional multi-asset formulations; systematic instability characterisation with more seeds and longer training.
