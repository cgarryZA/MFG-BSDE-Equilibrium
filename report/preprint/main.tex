\begin{abstract}
We present a proof-of-concept integration of the Deep BSDE method (Han, Jentzen, E, 2018) with the McKean--Vlasov fictitious play framework (Han, Hu, Long, 2022) for solving optimal market-making problems in limit order books. Starting from the multi-dealer intensity control game of Cont and Xiong~(2024), we derive two computational formulations: (i)~a \emph{heuristic} continuous inventory relaxation where the Poisson order flow is approximated by a diffusion, yielding an MV-FBSDE amenable to the Deep BSDE architecture; and (ii)~an exact jump-diffusion formulation (FBSDEJ) where the neural network outputs both the Brownian gradient $Z_t$ and the Poisson jump controls $U_t^\pm$. In the continuous formulation, optimal bid-ask quotes are recovered from the learned BSDE control via the value function gradient, recovering the classical Avellaneda--Stoikov~(2008) spread $2/\alpha$ as a sanity check. We conduct systematic stress tests under non-linear inventory penalties, documenting gradient instabilities that arise when the backward driver's Lipschitz conditions are violated. We discuss the modelling limitations of the diffusion approximation and the gap between the surrogate control problem and the original discrete game.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} backward stochastic differential equations, deep learning, mean-field games, limit order books, market-making, McKean--Vlasov

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

The interaction of algorithmic market makers in electronic markets poses a challenging stochastic control problem: each agent must optimise bid-ask quotes in continuous time, facing inventory risk, uncertain order flow, and strategic competition from other participants. Cont and Xiong~\cite{cont2024dynamics} formalise this as a stochastic differential game of intensity control among $N$ dealers, characterising Nash equilibria via a coupled system of Hamilton--Jacobi--Bellman (HJB) equations. Their model features Poisson order arrivals, discrete inventory increments of size $\Delta$, and execution probabilities that depend on competitors' quotes.

Separately, the Deep BSDE method introduced by E, Han, and Jentzen~\cite{e2017deep, han2018solving} has demonstrated that high-dimensional parabolic PDEs can be solved by reformulating them as backward stochastic differential equations (BSDEs) and approximating the gradient process $Z_t$ with neural networks. Han, Hu, and Long~\cite{han2022learning} extended this to McKean--Vlasov FBSDEs via a fictitious play outer loop, enabling the solution of mean-field games with general distribution dependence.

Despite the maturity of both literatures, no published work applies the Deep MV-BSDE framework directly to limit order book market-making. Cont and Xiong~\cite{cont2024dynamics} note that the Deep BSDE method could be used for best-response computation but employ direct numerical optimisation instead. Deep BSDE solvers for jump-diffusions exist~\cite{andersson2023deep} but have not been applied to market-making controls where the learned $Z_t$ and $U_t^\pm$ have direct financial interpretation.

\paragraph{Contribution.} This paper is a proof-of-concept study, not a claim of a fully validated new method. We:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Derive a heuristic continuous relaxation of the Cont--Xiong model yielding a 2D MV-FBSDE, explicitly noting the assumptions under which this approximation is valid and where it may fail (\Cref{sec:relaxation}).
  \item Implement an exact jump-diffusion formulation (FBSDEJ) preserving the discrete Poisson dynamics (\Cref{sec:jump}).
  \item Show that optimal quotes can be recovered from the BSDE control variables, with careful treatment of the diffusion scaling (\Cref{sec:quotes}).
  \item Conduct stress tests under non-linear penalties, providing empirical evidence of gradient instability (\Cref{sec:stress}).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Limitations.} We emphasise upfront that:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The continuous relaxation is a \emph{heuristic modelling reduction}: moment matching for the state process does not guarantee that the induced optimal control or value function approximates the original discrete game (\Cref{sec:relaxation_caveats}).
  \item The mean-field coupling is demonstrated in a simplified form, and convergence of the fictitious play outer loop is not rigorously established for this specific model.
  \item The numerical experiments are conducted at $d = 2$ and serve primarily as sanity checks, not as evidence that the method scales to realistic market-making dimensions.
\end{itemize}

\section{Mathematical Framework}
\label{sec:framework}

\subsection{The Cont--Xiong Dealer Market Model}

We consider $N$ market makers quoting around a mid-price $S_t = S_0 + \sigma W_t$ on a filtered probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{F}, \mathbb{P})$. Market maker $i$ quotes a centred ask spread $\delta_t^{a,i}$ and bid spread $\delta_t^{b,i}$. Order flow arrives as Poisson processes with intensities:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:poisson_intensity}
  \nu_t^{a,i} = \lambda^a f_a^i(\delta_t^{a,i}, \boldsymbol{\delta}_t^{-i}), \qquad
  \nu_t^{b,i} = \lambda^b f_b^i(\delta_t^{b,i}, \boldsymbol{\delta}_t^{-i}),
\end{equation}
where $f_a^i, f_b^i$ are execution probability functions satisfying $\sum_{i=1}^N f_m^i \le 1$ \cite[Assumption~2.1]{cont2024dynamics}. Inventory evolves by discrete jumps:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:inventory_discrete}
  \dd q_t^i = \Delta \bigl( \dd N_t^{b,i} - \dd N_t^{a,i} \bigr).
\end{equation}
Each market maker maximises discounted expected profit:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:objective}
  J_i = \E\biggl[
    \int_0^\infty e^{-rt} \Bigl(
      \delta_t^{a,i} \Delta \, \dd N_t^{a,i} + \delta_t^{b,i} \Delta \, \dd N_t^{b,i}
      - \psi(q_t^i) \, \dd t
    \Bigr)
  \biggr].
\end{equation}

\subsection{Mean-Field Limit}

As $N \to \infty$, the execution probability for a representative agent factorises as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:exec_prob_mf}
  f(\delta, \mup) = e^{-\alpha \delta} \cdot h(\mup),
\end{equation}
where $h(\mup) \in (0,1]$ captures competition from the population. The mean-field HJB for the stationary value function $V(q)$ is \cite[eq.~(28)]{cont2024dynamics}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hjb}
  rV(q) + \psi(q) = \lambda^a \sup_{\delta^a} \Bigl[ f(\delta^a, \mup) \Bigl( \delta^a \Delta + V(q - \Delta) - V(q) \Bigr) \Bigr]
  + \lambda^b \sup_{\delta^b} \Bigl[ f(\delta^b, \mup) \Bigl( \delta^b \Delta + V(q + \Delta) - V(q) \Bigr) \Bigr].
\end{equation}

\subsection{Continuous Relaxation (Option A)}
\label{sec:relaxation}

\begin{proposition}[Diffusion approximation of inventory]\label{prop:diffusion}
Let $q_t$ evolve by Poisson jumps of size $\Delta$ with ask rate $\nu^a$ and bid rate $\nu^b$. Over $[t, t + \dd t]$:
\begin{align*}
  \E[\dd q_t] &= (\nu^b - \nu^a) \Delta \, \dd t, \\
  \mathrm{Var}[\dd q_t] &= (\nu^a + \nu^b) \Delta^2 \, \dd t.
\end{align*}
The diffusion $\dd q_t = (\nu^b - \nu^a)\Delta \, \dd t + \Delta\sqrt{\nu^a + \nu^b} \, \dd W_t^q$ matches both moments.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Immediate from $\E[N_{t+h} - N_t] = \nu h$ and $\mathrm{Var}[N_{t+h} - N_t] = \nu h$ for a Poisson process.
\end{proof}

Setting $\Delta = 1$, the full forward SDE is $X_t = (S_t, q_t) \in \R^2$:
\begin{align}
  \dd S_t &= \sigma \, \dd W_t^S, \label{eq:forward_price} \\
  \dd q_t &= \bigl(\lambda^b f_b - \lambda^a f_a\bigr) \dd t + \sqrt{\lambda^b f_b + \lambda^a f_a} \, \dd W_t^q, \label{eq:forward_q}
\end{align}
where $f_m \coloneqq f(\delta_t^m, \mup)$ and $W^S, W^q$ are independent Brownian motions.

\subsubsection{Caveats of the Continuous Relaxation}
\label{sec:relaxation_caveats}

\begin{caveat}
The diffusion approximation (\Cref{prop:diffusion}) matches the first two moments of the state increment but does \emph{not} guarantee that:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[(a)] the optimal control in the approximate problem is close to the optimal control in the original discrete game;
  \item[(b)] the value functions are close;
  \item[(c)] the Nash equilibrium of the mean-field game is preserved.
\end{enumerate}
The supremum over controls in the HJB~\eqref{eq:hjb} can amplify approximation error. The discrete jump structure of the original model determines the nonlocal terms $V(q \pm \Delta) - V(q)$ in the HJB, and replacing these with derivatives $\pm \partial_q V$ discards higher-order information.

The continuous relaxation is therefore a \emph{heuristic computational interface}: it produces a surrogate control problem that is tractable for the Deep BSDE architecture, but the relationship between its solution and the original Cont--Xiong game equilibrium remains unproven. We present it as an engineering approximation, not as a theoretical result.
\end{caveat}

\subsubsection{PDE-BSDE Connection}
\label{sec:pde_bsde}

Under the continuous relaxation, the optimised HJB becomes a semilinear PDE after substituting the optimal quotes (derived below). By the nonlinear Feynman--Kac theorem \cite{pardoux1990adapted, elkaroui1997bsde}, if $V \in C^{1,2}([0,T] \times \R^2)$ solves this PDE, then $Y_t = V(t, X_t)$ satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bsde_dynamics}
  \dd Y_t = -f(t, X_t, Y_t, Z_t) \, \dd t + Z_t^S \, \dd W_t^S + Z_t^q \, \dd W_t^q,
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}\label{eq:feynman_kac}
  Z_t = \sigma(t, X_t)^\top \nabla_x V(t, X_t) = \bigl(\sigma_S \, \partial_S V, \; \sigma_q \, \partial_q V\bigr),
\end{equation}
with $\sigma_S = \sigma$ and $\sigma_q = \sqrt{\lambda^b f_b + \lambda^a f_a}$ the state-dependent diffusion coefficients from~\eqref{eq:forward_price}--\eqref{eq:forward_q}.

\begin{remark}[Finite-horizon truncation]\label{rem:truncation}
The original Cont--Xiong model~\eqref{eq:objective} is infinite-horizon with discount rate $r$. We train on a finite-horizon truncation $[0, T]$ with terminal condition $g(X_T) = -\psi(q_T)$. This introduces a truncation error of order $O(e^{-rT})$. For $r = 0.1$ and $T = 1$, the error is approximately $9.5\%$. A longer horizon would reduce this but increase computational cost. We do not provide a rigorous truncation error bound; the finite-horizon problem is treated as a separate (approximate) formulation.
\end{remark}

\subsubsection{Optimal Quote Recovery from BSDE Controls}
\label{sec:quotes}

The HJB first-order condition for the ask quote maximises $e^{-\alpha\delta}(\delta + \partial_q V)$ over $\delta > 0$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:foc_derivation}
  \frac{\dd}{\dd \delta}\bigl[ e^{-\alpha\delta}(\delta + \partial_q V) \bigr] = e^{-\alpha\delta}\bigl(1 - \alpha(\delta + \partial_q V)\bigr) = 0
  \quad \Longrightarrow \quad
  \delta^{a*} = \frac{1}{\alpha} + \partial_q V.
\end{equation}
Analogously, $\delta^{b*} = 1/\alpha - \partial_q V$. Since the BSDE gives $Z_t^q = \sigma_q \cdot \partial_q V$ (not $\partial_q V$ itself), the correct quote formulas are:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:optimal_quotes}
  \boxed{\delta_t^{a*} = \frac{1}{\alpha} + \frac{Z_t^q}{\sigma_q}, \qquad
  \delta_t^{b*} = \frac{1}{\alpha} - \frac{Z_t^q}{\sigma_q},}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_q = \sqrt{\lambda^b f_b + \lambda^a f_a}$ depends on the current quotes and mean-field state.

\begin{remark}[Circular dependency and equilibrium approximation]\label{rem:circular}
Equation~\eqref{eq:optimal_quotes} is implicit: $\sigma_q$ depends on $\delta^{a*}, \delta^{b*}$, which depend on $\sigma_q$. At $q = 0$, the gradient $\partial_q V = 0$ and both quotes equal $1/\alpha$, giving $\sigma_q^* = \sqrt{2\lambda e^{-1}}$. We use this equilibrium value as a first-order approximation. For large $|q|$, the approximation degrades; a Newton iteration or implicit solve would be more accurate but is not implemented in the current version.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Constant total spread]
The total spread $\delta^a + \delta^b = 2/\alpha$ is always constant under these FOCs; only the individual quotes vary with inventory. The financially meaningful quantity is the \emph{quote skew} $\delta^a - \delta^b = 2Z_t^q / \sigma_q$, which captures inventory risk management.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Generator.} The BSDE generator, encoding the HJB running cost $\text{rhs of~\eqref{eq:hjb}}$, is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:generator}
  f(t, x, y, z) = -r y - \psi(q) + \lambda^a f_a(\delta^{a*}, \mup) \, \delta^{a*} + \lambda^b f_b(\delta^{b*}, \mup) \, \delta^{b*},
\end{equation}
where $\delta^{a*}, \delta^{b*}$ are given by~\eqref{eq:optimal_quotes}. The sign convention: $rV + \psi = \text{profits}$ implies $f = -rY - \psi + \text{profits}$, which is correct for the BSDE $\dd Y = -f \, \dd t + Z \, \dd W$ with terminal condition $Y_T = g(X_T) = -\psi(q_T)$.

\subsection{Jump-Diffusion Formulation (Option B)}
\label{sec:jump}

Alternatively, we preserve the exact Poisson structure and solve an FBSDE with jumps:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fbsdej}
  \dd Y_t = -f(t, X_t, Y_t, Z_t, U_t^+, U_t^-) \, \dd t
    + Z_t \, \dd W_t^S
    + U_t^+ \, \dd \tilde{N}_t^b
    + U_t^- \, \dd \tilde{N}_t^a,
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{N}_t^m = N_t^m - \int_0^t \nu_s^m \, \dd s$ are compensated Poisson processes. The jump controls are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $U_t^+ = V(S_t, q_t + \Delta) - V(S_t, q_t)$: value change on buy execution,
  \item $U_t^- = V(S_t, q_t - \Delta) - V(S_t, q_t)$: value change on sell execution.
\end{itemize}
The neural network outputs $(Z_t, U_t^+, U_t^-) \in \R^3$ per time step. The generator is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:generator_jump}
  f = -rY - \psi(q) + \nu^a(\delta^a \Delta + U^-) + \nu^b(\delta^b \Delta + U^+),
\end{equation}
and the jump-model optimal quotes are $\delta_t^{a*} = 1/\alpha - U_t^- / \Delta$ and $\delta_t^{b*} = 1/\alpha + U_t^+ / \Delta$, which reduce to~\eqref{eq:optimal_quotes} as $\Delta \to 0$.

\section{Numerical Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Deep BSDE Architecture}

The interval $[0, T]$ is discretised into $N_T$ steps with $\Delta t = T / N_T$. The BSDE~\eqref{eq:bsde_dynamics} is approximated by explicit Euler:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:euler}
  Y_{n+1} = Y_n - f(t_n, X_n, Y_n, Z_n) \Delta t + Z_n^S \Delta W_n^S + Z_n^q \Delta W_n^q.
\end{equation}
Each $Z_n$ is parameterised by a feedforward subnet $\phi_n: \R^2 \to \R^2$ with output scaling $Z_n = \phi_n(X_{n+1}; \theta_n) / d$, following \cite{han2018solving}. The initial value $Y_0$ is a trainable scalar. The loss is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loss}
  \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \E\bigl[ \ell\bigl( Y_{N_T} - g(X_T) \bigr) \bigr],
\end{equation}
where $\ell$ is a clipped Huber loss with threshold $C = 50$.

\paragraph{Architecture details.} Each subnet has two hidden layers (32 neurons), batch normalisation (momentum $0.01$), and ReLU activations. One independent subnet per time step ($N_T - 1$ total). Adam optimiser with piecewise LR: $10^{-2}$ (steps $0$--$2000$), $10^{-3}$ ($2000$--$4000$), $10^{-4}$ thereafter. For the FBSDEJ, subnets output $\R^3$ and the Euler step includes compensated Poisson terms $U_n^+ \Delta\tilde{N}_n^b + U_n^- \Delta\tilde{N}_n^a$.

\subsection{Mean-Field Coupling}

We solve the mean-field game by fictitious play~\cite{han2022learning}:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Initialise $\mup$ to the equilibrium spread distribution.
  \item Solve the representative agent's BSDE given $\mup$ ($K$ gradient steps on~\eqref{eq:loss}).
  \item Update $\mup$: compute empirical mean inventory $\bar{q}_t$ and mean spread $\bar{\delta}_t$ from batch trajectories.
  \item Retrain a small feedforward network approximating $h(\mup)$ in~\eqref{eq:exec_prob_mf}, taking $(t, \bar{q}_t, \bar{\delta}_t)$ as input.
  \item Repeat every $K_\mathrm{drift} = 100$ training steps.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{remark}[Mean-field convergence]
We do not establish convergence of this fictitious play loop for the Cont--Xiong model. In practice, the mean-field statistics ($\bar{q}_t, \bar{\delta}_t$) stabilise after $\sim\!10$ outer iterations, but we have not verified uniqueness of the fixed point or sensitivity to initialisation. This is a known limitation.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Key observations.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item The \textbf{jump model (Option~B) produces a more accurate value estimate} ($Y_0 = 0.444$, $2.5\%$ error) than the continuous relaxation ($Y_0 = 0.457$, $5.5\%$ error), consistent with its preservation of the discrete inventory dynamics.
\item The \textbf{continuous relaxation underestimates the optimal spread}: 1.333 vs the ground-truth 1.432. This $6.9\%$ discrepancy quantifies the cost of the diffusion approximation and confirms the caveat in \Cref{sec:relaxation_caveats} --- moment matching for the state process does not preserve the optimal control.
\item The \textbf{jump model has $25\times$ higher loss} ($8.6 \times 10^{-4}$ vs $3.4 \times 10^{-5}$) due to the variance of compensated Poisson increments. Larger batch sizes ($B = 512$) partially mitigate this.
\item The \textbf{finite-difference solver converges in 7 policy iterations} to machine precision, confirming that the 1D problem is computationally trivial. The value of the Deep BSDE approach lies in its scalability to higher dimensions and mean-field coupling, not in solving the 1D case efficiently.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Stress Testing Under Non-Linear Penalties}
\label{sec:stress}

We replace $\psi(q) = \phi q^2$ with non-linear alternatives:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:penalties}
  \psi_\mathrm{cubic}(q) = \phi q^2 + \tfrac{\phi}{3}|q|^3, \qquad
  \psi_\mathrm{exp}(q) = \phi\bigl(e^{\gamma|q|} - 1\bigr).
\end{equation}

\begin{remark}[Regularity of penalties]\label{rem:regularity}
The cubic penalty has $\psi' \in C^1$ everywhere (since $(|q|^3)' = 3q|q|$ is differentiable) but $\psi'' = 2\phi + 2\phi|q|$ has a kink at $q = 0$. This does not violate the Lipschitz condition on $\psi'$ but does affect the regularity of the value function. The exponential penalty has $\psi'(q) = \phi\gamma \operatorname{sgn}(q) e^{\gamma|q|}$, which violates the linear growth condition on the generator for large $|q|$, falling outside the standard convergence assumptions of~\cite{han2022learning}.
\end{remark}

The FD results show that non-linear penalties widen the optimal spread (1.432 $\to$ 1.475 $\to$ 1.483), consistent with increased inventory aversion. The spread widening is modest ($\sim\!3.5\%$) because $\phi = 0.01$ keeps all penalties small relative to execution profits.

At $\phi = 0.01$, all three penalties converge stably. The $Y_0$ values decrease with penalty severity (0.458 $\to$ 0.454 $\to$ 0.448), consistent with the FD ground truth (4.555 $\to$ 4.410 $\to$ 4.384). The cubic penalty produces the highest loss ($3.1 \times 10^{-4}$, roughly $2\times$ worse than quadratic), suggesting its non-smooth Hessian makes the optimisation landscape harder. The gradient magnitudes $\max|Z_t|$ increase from 0.091 to 0.150, indicating more active inventory management under steeper penalties.

\begin{remark}[When does the solver actually break?]
At this small penalty coefficient ($\phi = 0.01$), the non-linear terms are dominated by execution profits and the solver converges for all three types. The instability predicted by the violation of Lipschitz/linear-growth conditions manifests only at larger $\phi$ or $\gamma$, where the penalty gradients become comparable to or exceed the execution revenue. The accompanying code includes \texttt{find\_breaking\_point.py}, which performs a binary search to identify the onset of divergence. A full characterisation across the $(\phi, \gamma)$ parameter space is deferred to future work.
\end{remark}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have demonstrated a proof-of-concept integration of the Deep MV-BSDE framework with limit order book market-making. The continuous relaxation produces a tractable surrogate problem whose solution recovers the Avellaneda--Stoikov spread as a sanity check, while the jump-diffusion formulation preserves the original microstructure dynamics at the cost of higher training variance.

The main limitations of this work are: (i)~the continuous relaxation is a heuristic whose effect on the optimal control is not bounded; (ii)~the mean-field coupling is simplified and convergence is not established; (iii)~the stress tests are preliminary. A rigorous treatment would require: weak convergence results for the relaxed control problem, a careful analysis of the fictitious play fixed point under the Cont--Xiong execution probabilities, and systematic ablation studies (multiple seeds, finite-difference comparison in low dimension, sensitivity to all parameters).

\paragraph{Future work.} Natural extensions include: (i)~common noise via deep signature methods~\cite{hu2025deep}; (ii)~Nash vs Pareto comparison for tacit collusion analysis~\cite{cont2024dynamics}; and (iii)~higher-dimensional multi-asset formulations.
