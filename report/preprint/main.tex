\begin{abstract}
We investigate when and why distribution-dependent mean-field coupling becomes learnable in deep BSDE solvers for optimal market-making. Starting from the Cont--Xiong (2024) dealer market model, we implement four law encoders (moments, quantiles, histogram, DeepSets) to represent the population distribution, and test whether the learned optimal control depends on population shape. We uncover three failure modes that can mask genuine mean-field effects: (i)~a broken coupling pathway where the BSDE generator does not use the law embedding; (ii)~BatchNorm systematically erasing broadcast (shared) features that are constant across the training batch; and (iii)~mean-pooled permutation-invariant encoders (DeepSets) failing to capture distributional variance under symmetric inputs. After correcting all three, we show that the competitive factor $h(\Phi(\mu_t))$ varies by $4\times$ across population shapes, optimal quotes shift by $2\times$, and quote skew flips sign --- demonstrating causally verified distribution-dependent controls. The effect is robust across encoder types (MomentEncoder and QuantileEncoder produce qualitatively identical results) and penalty regimes ($\phi = 0.01$ to $0.5$). We extend the model with adverse selection (price-dependent execution), breaking the dimensional reduction that made the base problem effectively one-dimensional, and map the stability frontier across penalty strength, adverse selection intensity, and coupling type. The central finding is that \emph{solver sensitivity to population structure depends critically on architecture and law representation}: mean-field effects are present in the model but invisible to the solver unless the law encoder exposes the relevant distributional features and the architecture preserves them through normalisation layers.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} mean-field games, backward stochastic differential equations, deep learning, market-making, law encoders, BatchNorm, representation collapse

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Mean-field game (MFG) and McKean--Vlasov (MV) formulations promise to capture the aggregate effects of interacting agents in large-population stochastic control problems. In market-making, this means each dealer's optimal quotes should depend not just on their own inventory but on the distribution of inventories and quotes across the population~\cite{cont2024dynamics, carmona2018probabilistic}.

Deep BSDE methods~\cite{han2018solving, e2017deep} offer a scalable computational framework for such problems, and Han, Hu, and Long~\cite{han2022learning} extended them to MV-FBSDEs via fictitious play. However, no published work has demonstrated that deep BSDE solvers can learn genuinely distribution-dependent controls in a market-making setting --- or identified the conditions under which they fail to do so.

In a companion preprint~\cite{garry2026preprint1}, we showed that a deep jump-BSDE solver achieves $<0.1\%$ value error against finite-difference ground truth on the Cont--Xiong model, but the mean-field coupling (implemented as a moment proxy) had no measurable effect on the learned control. We attributed this to the weak-coupling regime ($\phi = 0.01$).

In this paper, we show that the true explanation is deeper: the mean-field channel was architecturally dead due to three independent failure modes, each sufficient to suppress the signal. After correcting all three, we demonstrate that:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Distribution-dependent competitive effects produce $4\times$ variation in fill rates and $2\times$ variation in optimal quotes across population shapes.
  \item The effect is robust across handcrafted law encoders (moments, quantiles) but invisible to mean-pooled DeepSets under symmetric inputs.
  \item Adverse selection (price-dependent execution) breaks the dimensional reduction $V = x + qs + \phi(t,q)$, making the problem genuinely multi-dimensional.
  \item The stability frontier shifts when mean-field coupling is active.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Contribution.} This is an empirical computational study. We do not prove convergence theorems. Our contribution is diagnostic: identifying \emph{when and why} mean-field structure becomes learnable, and demonstrating that representation choice is the critical bottleneck.

\section{Model and Formulation}
\label{sec:model}

We work with the Cont--Xiong dealer market model~\cite{cont2024dynamics} as described in~\cite{garry2026preprint1}. The key ingredients are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Mid-price $S_t = S_0 + \sigma W_t$ (driftless Brownian).
  \item Inventory $q_t$ evolving by Poisson jumps (or diffusion approximation).
  \item Execution probability $f(\delta, \mup) = e^{-\alpha\delta} \cdot h(\mup)$, where $h$ captures population competition.
  \item Objective: maximise discounted spread profits minus inventory penalty $\psi(q)$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Mean-Field Coupling via Law Encoders}

In~\cite{garry2026preprint1}, the competitive factor $h(\mup)$ was approximated by a moment proxy $\hat{h}(t, \bar{q}_t, \bar{\delta}_t)$ trained on mean inventory and mean spread. This produced no measurable effect.

We replace this with a \emph{learned} competitive factor:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:h_law}
  h(\mup) = g\bigl(\Phi(\mup)\bigr), \qquad \Phi(\mup) = \text{LawEncoder}(\{(S_i, q_i)\}_{i=1}^N),
\end{equation}
where $\Phi$ maps the empirical population to a fixed-size embedding and $g: \R^d \to (0, 1]$ is a learned competitive factor network (sigmoid output). Four encoders are tested:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{MomentEncoder}: mean, variance, skewness, mean$|q|$, max$|q|$, std $\to \R^6$.
  \item \textbf{QuantileEncoder}: mean + 5 quantiles $\to \R^6$.
  \item \textbf{HistogramEncoder}: soft Gaussian histogram $\to \R^{20}$.
  \item \textbf{DeepSetsEncoder}: $\Phi = \rho(\frac{1}{N}\sum_i \psi(x_i))$ with learned $\psi, \rho$ $\to \R^{16}$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Adverse Selection Extension}

To break the dimensional reduction $V = x + qs + \phi(t,q)$ proved in~\cite{garry2026preprint1}, we introduce price-dependent execution:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:adverse}
  f_a(\delta, \text{signal}) = e^{-\alpha\delta}(1 + \eta \cdot \text{signal}), \qquad
  f_b(\delta, \text{signal}) = e^{-\alpha\delta}(1 - \eta \cdot \text{signal}),
\end{equation}
where $\text{signal}_t$ is an EMA of normalised price increments and $\eta$ controls adverse selection strength. The state becomes $(S, q, \text{signal}) \in \R^3$.

\section{Three Failure Modes of Mean-Field Coupling}
\label{sec:failures}

We identified three independent architectural failures, each sufficient to suppress the mean-field signal.

\subsection{Failure 1: Generator Bypass}

The BSDE generator $f(t, x, y, z)$ computes execution profits and thus determines the training loss. In our initial implementation, $f$ inherited from the base class and used the old scalar moment proxy $\hat{h}(t, \bar{q}, \bar{\delta})$ rather than the law embedding $\Phi(\mup)$. The law encoder entered the subnet inputs (affecting $Z$ prediction) but not the generator (affecting the BSDE dynamics). Since the loss is determined by the terminal mismatch $|Y_T - g(X_T)|^2$ propagated through the generator, the optimiser had no gradient signal connecting $\Phi$ to the loss.

\paragraph{Fix.} Override $f$ to compute $h = g(\Phi(\mup))$ and use it in execution probabilities. Store the current law embedding via \texttt{set\_current\_law\_embed()} before each $f$ evaluation.

\subsection{Failure 2: BatchNorm Erasure of Broadcast Features}

The law embedding $\Phi(\mup)$ is identical for all agents in the batch at a given time step (it represents the population, not the individual). BatchNorm normalises across the batch dimension:
\[
  \text{BN}(x) = \frac{x - \E_{\text{batch}}[x]}{\sqrt{\text{Var}_{\text{batch}}(x) + \epsilon}}.
\]
When $x$ is constant across the batch, $\text{Var}_{\text{batch}} = 0$ and the output is zero (or near-zero from $\epsilon$). This erasure persists through all subsequent layers because the law contribution remains constant-like after mixing.

\paragraph{Fix.} Two-stream architecture: state features pass through BatchNorm, law features bypass it entirely. The streams are combined additively after separate processing.

\subsection{Failure 3: DeepSets Representation Collapse}

The DeepSets encoder computes $\Phi = \rho(\frac{1}{N}\sum_i \psi(x_i))$. Under random initialisation, $\psi$ is approximately symmetric. For zero-mean populations, positive and negative inventory contributions cancel under mean pooling, collapsing variance information:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Encoder & Embedding distance & Cosine similarity \\
& (std 0.1 vs 5.0) & \\
\midrule
DeepSets & 0.003 & 1.000 \\
MomentEncoder & 24.98 & varies \\
QuantileEncoder & 9.84 & varies \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The DeepSets encoder produces nearly identical embeddings for populations with radically different variance. Under symmetric inputs and random initialisation, mean pooling suppresses variance information, making discrimination difficult without explicit variance-sensitive features.

\paragraph{Implication.} Permutation-invariant encoders based on mean pooling may fail to capture higher-order distributional properties under symmetric inputs unless the per-element network $\psi$ is explicitly designed or trained to break this symmetry. This is a representation limitation, not a coupling failure.

\section{Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

\subsection{Two-Stream Mean-Field Subnet}

To prevent BatchNorm suppression while maintaining training stability:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{State stream}: $\text{BN} \to \text{Dense} \to \text{BN} \to \text{ReLU} \to \text{Dense}$ (standard Deep BSDE architecture).
  \item \textbf{Law stream}: $\text{Dense} \to \text{ReLU} \to \text{Dense}$ (no normalisation).
  \item \textbf{Output}: $\text{state\_output} + \text{law\_output}$ (additive).
\end{itemize}

The law embedding survives all layers because it never passes through BatchNorm.

\subsection{Competitive Factor Network}

$g: \R^d \to (0.1, 1]$ maps the law embedding to a scalar competitive factor via $\text{Dense} \to \text{ReLU} \to \text{Dense} \to \text{Sigmoid}$, with a floor of $0.1$ to prevent degenerate zero-execution regimes. Note that this floor compresses the lower end of the $h$ range: several ``wide'' population results in \Cref{tab:control_sensitivity,tab:phi_sweep} hit $h = 0.100$ exactly, indicating saturation. The reported gaps may therefore underestimate the true variation.

\subsection{Training Details}

All models use float64 precision, Adam optimiser with piecewise constant LR ($10^{-2}$ for steps 0--2000, $10^{-3}$ for 2000--4000, $10^{-4}$ thereafter), batch size 256, 50 Euler time steps over $T = 1.0$. State stream: 2 hidden layers $\times$ 32 neurons with BatchNorm (momentum 0.01). Law stream: 2 hidden layers $\times$ 32 neurons, no normalisation. Competitive factor network $g$: Linear(embed\_dim $\to$ 16) $\to$ ReLU $\to$ Linear(16 $\to$ 1) $\to$ Sigmoid, with output $0.1 + 0.9 \cdot \text{sigmoid}(\cdot)$ to enforce $h \in (0.1, 1]$. Population shapes are sampled as $q_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_q^2)$ clipped to $[-10, 10]$. Training uses the batch itself as the particle system --- no separate simulation. All code: \url{https://github.com/cgarryZA/DeepMVBSDEJ}.

\begin{remark}[Quote admissibility]
At extreme population dispersion (std $= 5.0$), the model produces negative ask spreads ($\delta_a < 0$), meaning the agent quotes inside the mid-price. This represents maximally aggressive selling to reduce inventory exposure under fierce competition. In the implementation, quotes are clipped to $[\epsilon, 10/\alpha]$, but the unclipped values are reported here to show the model's unconstrained policy.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Causal and Consistency Checks}

\paragraph{Placebo test: shuffled embeddings.}
To confirm that the learned sensitivity is genuinely driven by population structure (not extra model capacity), we destroy the semantic link between the population and its embedding while preserving architecture and dimensionality (\Cref{tab:placebo}).

\begin{table}[!htb]
\centering

\label{tab:placebo}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
Embedding source & $h$ value & Interpretation \\
\midrule
Real narrow (std 0.1) & 0.434 & Genuine signal \\

\midrule

Shuffled and random embeddings all produce $h \approx 0.44$--$0.47$, similar to the narrow (low-competition) baseline. They do \emph{not} produce $h = 0.10$ (the wide-population value). The low $h$ under wide populations is therefore genuinely caused by the distributional signal, not by random embedding noise or extra model capacity. Since the architecture, dimensionality, and training procedure are unchanged, the collapse of the effect under shuffled and synthetic embeddings isolates the semantic content of the law representation as the sole driver.

\paragraph{Swap test.}
Feeding the narrow embedding to an agent at $q = 0$ gives $\delta_a = 0.66$ (conservative); feeding the wide embedding gives $\delta_a = 0.30$ (aggressive). Same agent state, different population embedding $\to$ different policy. This confirms the control pathway is causally active.

The neural network's larger quote variation arises from the law embedding entering the subnet directly (two-stream architecture), providing an additional pathway for law-dependent variation beyond the standard HJB sensitivity to execution rates.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Representation determines learnability.}
The central finding is that mean-field effects are \emph{present in the model} but \emph{invisible to the solver} unless the law encoder exposes the relevant distributional features. This has implications beyond this specific problem: any deep learning system that conditions on shared population statistics via broadcast features must account for normalisation layer interactions.

\paragraph{Architectural lessons.}
BatchNorm, while critical for Deep BSDE training stability~\cite{han2018solving}, is fundamentally incompatible with broadcast conditioning signals. The two-stream architecture (BN on per-agent features, no BN on shared features) resolves this cleanly. FiLM-style modulation~\cite{perez2018film} would be a natural alternative. More broadly, these findings suggest that any deep learning system conditioning on shared population statistics must carefully consider representation and normalisation, as architectural choices can mask genuine interaction effects.

\paragraph{Economic interpretation.}
The learned competitive factor $h(\Phi(\mup))$ maps wider inventory distributions to lower fill rates, consistent with the intuition that dispersed populations contain more aggressive competitors. The resulting policy adaptations (narrower ask under competition, wider bid) are economically rational inventory management responses.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have shown that deep BSDE solvers can be made sensitive to population structure in market-making, producing economically meaningful law-conditioned controls, but only when three architectural conditions are met: the law embedding enters the BSDE generator, broadcast features bypass BatchNorm, and the encoder captures distributional variance. When all three are satisfied, the solver learns distribution-dependent controls that disappear under semantic-destruction placebos and align directionally with finite-difference comparative statics under exogenous competition.

The deeper lesson is methodological: null results in mean-field deep learning may reflect architectural failures rather than genuine model invariance. Diagnosing the failure mode --- coupling pathway, normalisation, or representation --- is essential before concluding that population structure does not matter.

\paragraph{Limitations.} We demonstrate learned sensitivity to population structure, not equilibrium-correct sensitivity: the competitive factor network learns a monotone mapping (wider $\to$ lower $h$) but we have not verified that this corresponds to a Nash equilibrium fixed point or that fictitious play converges. Multi-seed results (\Cref{tab:multiseed}) confirm the $h$-gap is robust ($0.259 \pm 0.055$), but the $Y_0$ magnitude has higher variance across seeds ($\pm 0.039$). The competitive factor floor at $h = 0.1$ compresses the wide-population regime and may affect reported gap magnitudes. The adverse selection model uses a simple EMA signal. The experiments are at $d = 2$--$3$. We leave out-of-distribution generalization across unseen population shapes to future work.

\paragraph{Future work.} Fictitious play convergence verification; FiLM conditioning as alternative to two-stream; DeepSets with variance-sensitive features ($q^2$ pooling); common noise via deep signatures~\cite{hu2025deep}.
