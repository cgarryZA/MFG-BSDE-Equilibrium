\begin{abstract}
We investigate the architectural conditions under which deep BSDE solvers can use population-level information in mean-field market-making problems. Starting from the Cont--Xiong (2024) dealer market model, we implement four law encoders (moments, quantiles, histogram, DeepSets) to represent the population distribution, and test whether the learned control depends on population shape. We identify two architectural failure modes that silently suppress mean-field effects: (i)~BatchNorm erasing broadcast features that are constant across the training batch; and (ii)~mean-pooled permutation-invariant encoders collapsing distributional variance under symmetric inputs. After correcting both, the competitive factor $h(\Phi(\mu_t))$ varies across population shapes and optimal quotes shift measurably. However, a controlled pathway-disentanglement experiment reveals that \emph{all} policy variation arises from direct conditioning of the policy subnet on population features, not from the BSDE generator channel: an $h$-only model learns economically sensible competitive factors (large variation) but produces identical controls and near-identical values. The central finding is that learning a correct economic signal in the generator is not sufficient for it to affect behaviour --- direct architectural pathways from law representation to control network are necessary. The effect is robust across encoder types but requires variance-sensitive features; mean-pooled DeepSets fails. We extend the model with adverse selection (price-dependent execution) and map the stability frontier across coupling type and penalty strength.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} mean-field games, backward stochastic differential equations, deep learning, market-making, law encoders, BatchNorm, representation collapse

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Mean-field game (MFG) and McKean--Vlasov (MV) formulations promise to capture the aggregate effects of interacting agents in large-population stochastic control problems. In market-making, this means each dealer's optimal quotes should depend not just on their own inventory but on the distribution of inventories and quotes across the population~\cite{cont2024dynamics, carmona2018probabilistic}.

Deep BSDE methods~\cite{han2018solving, e2017deep} offer a scalable computational framework for such problems, and Han, Hu, and Long~\cite{han2022learning} extended them to MV-FBSDEs via fictitious play. However, no published work has systematically investigated the architectural conditions under which deep BSDE solvers can incorporate population-level information into the learned control in a market-making setting.

In a companion preprint~\cite{garry2026preprint1}, we showed that a deep jump-BSDE solver achieves $<0.1\%$ value error against finite-difference ground truth on the Cont--Xiong model, but the mean-field coupling (implemented as a scalar moment proxy) had no measurable effect on the learned control.

In this paper, we show that the explanation is architectural: two independent failure modes, each sufficient on its own, suppressed the mean-field signal. After correcting both, we find:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The solver learns population-dependent competitive factors and quotes when law features directly condition the policy network. The effect is robust across encoder types but requires variance-sensitive features.
  \item However, the BSDE generator pathway alone is insufficient: a model where the law embedding enters only the generator learns correct competitive factors (large variation) but produces identical controls and near-identical values. Policy dependence arises entirely from direct subnet conditioning.
  \item BatchNorm systematically erases broadcast features; mean-pooled DeepSets collapses distributional variance under symmetric inputs. Both are silent failure modes that produce false-negative results about mean-field coupling.
  \item Adverse selection breaks the dimensional reduction, and the stability frontier shifts with coupling type.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Contribution.} This is an empirical computational study. We do not prove convergence theorems or verify Nash equilibrium. Our contribution is diagnostic: identifying the architectural conditions under which mean-field information becomes usable in deep BSDE solvers, and showing that without direct conditioning pathways, the signal does not propagate to controls even when correctly learned in the generator.

\section{Model and Formulation}
\label{sec:model}

We work with the Cont--Xiong dealer market model~\cite{cont2024dynamics} as described in~\cite{garry2026preprint1}. The key ingredients are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Mid-price $S_t = S_0 + \sigma W_t$ (driftless Brownian).
  \item Inventory $q_t$ evolving by Poisson jumps (or diffusion approximation).
  \item Execution probability $f(\delta, \mup) = e^{-\alpha\delta} \cdot h(\mup)$, where $h$ captures population competition.
  \item Objective: maximise discounted spread profits minus inventory penalty $\psi(q)$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Mean-Field Coupling via Law Encoders}

In~\cite{garry2026preprint1}, the competitive factor $h(\mup)$ was approximated by a scalar moment proxy $\hat{h}(t, \bar{q}_t, \bar{\delta}_t)$ based on mean inventory and mean spread. This produced no measurable effect on the learned control, which we attributed to the weak-coupling regime. Here we show that the failure was architectural.

We replace this with a \emph{learned} competitive factor:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:h_law}
  h(\mup) = g\bigl(\Phi(\mup)\bigr), \qquad \Phi(\mup) = \text{LawEncoder}(\{(S_i, q_i)\}_{i=1}^N),
\end{equation}
where $\Phi$ maps the empirical population to a fixed-size embedding and $g: \R^d \to (0, 1]$ is a learned competitive factor network (sigmoid output). Four encoders are tested:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{MomentEncoder}: mean, variance, skewness, mean$|q|$, max$|q|$, std $\to \R^6$.
  \item \textbf{QuantileEncoder}: mean + 5 quantiles $\to \R^6$.
  \item \textbf{HistogramEncoder}: soft Gaussian histogram $\to \R^{20}$.
  \item \textbf{DeepSetsEncoder}: $\Phi = \rho(\frac{1}{N}\sum_i \psi(x_i))$ with learned $\psi, \rho$ $\to \R^{16}$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Adverse Selection Extension}

To break the dimensional reduction $V = x + qs + \phi(t,q)$ proved in~\cite{garry2026preprint1}, we introduce price-dependent execution:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:adverse}
  f_a(\delta, \text{signal}) = e^{-\alpha\delta}(1 + \eta \cdot \text{signal}), \qquad
  f_b(\delta, \text{signal}) = e^{-\alpha\delta}(1 - \eta \cdot \text{signal}),
\end{equation}
where $\text{signal}_t$ is an EMA of normalised price increments and $\eta$ controls adverse selection strength. The state becomes $(S, q, \text{signal}) \in \R^3$.

\section{Two Architectural Failure Modes}
\label{sec:failures}

We identified two independent architectural issues that suppressed the mean-field signal, each sufficient on its own to render the coupling invisible. Both arise in correctly wired systems and have implications beyond this specific application.

\subsection{Failure 1: BatchNorm Erasure of Broadcast Features}

The law embedding $\Phi(\mup)$ is identical for all agents in the batch at a given time step (it represents the population, not the individual). BatchNorm normalises across the batch dimension:
\[
  \text{BN}(x) = \frac{x - \E_{\text{batch}}[x]}{\sqrt{\text{Var}_{\text{batch}}(x) + \epsilon}}.
\]
When $x$ is constant across the batch, $\text{Var}_{\text{batch}} = 0$ and the output is zero (or near-zero from $\epsilon$). This erasure persists through all subsequent layers because the law contribution remains constant-like after mixing.

\paragraph{Fix.} Two-stream architecture: state features pass through BatchNorm, law features bypass it entirely. The streams are combined additively after separate processing.

\subsection{Failure 2: Mean-Pooling Representation Collapse}

The DeepSets encoder computes $\Phi = \rho(\frac{1}{N}\sum_i \psi(x_i))$. Under random initialisation, $\psi$ is approximately symmetric. For zero-mean populations, positive and negative inventory contributions cancel under mean pooling, collapsing variance information:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Encoder & Embedding distance & Cosine similarity \\
& (std 0.1 vs 5.0) & \\
\midrule
DeepSets & 0.003 & 1.000 \\
MomentEncoder & 24.98 & varies \\
QuantileEncoder & 9.84 & varies \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The DeepSets encoder produces nearly identical embeddings for populations with radically different variance. Under symmetric inputs and random initialisation, mean pooling suppresses variance information, making discrimination difficult without explicit variance-sensitive features.

\paragraph{Implication.} Permutation-invariant encoders based on mean pooling may fail to capture higher-order distributional properties under symmetric inputs unless the per-element network $\psi$ is explicitly designed or trained to break this symmetry. This is a representation limitation, not a coupling failure.

\section{Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

\subsection{Two-Stream Mean-Field Subnet}

To prevent BatchNorm suppression while maintaining training stability:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{State stream}: $\text{BN} \to \text{Dense} \to \text{BN} \to \text{ReLU} \to \text{Dense}$ (standard Deep BSDE architecture).
  \item \textbf{Law stream}: $\text{Dense} \to \text{ReLU} \to \text{Dense}$ (no normalisation).
  \item \textbf{Output}: $\text{state\_output} + \text{law\_output}$ (additive).
\end{itemize}

The law embedding survives all layers because it never passes through BatchNorm.

\subsection{Competitive Factor Network}

$g: \R^d \to (0.01, 1]$ maps the law embedding to a scalar competitive factor via $\text{Dense} \to \text{ReLU} \to \text{Dense} \to \text{Sigmoid}$, with a minimal floor of $0.01$ to prevent exact zero execution. The floor does not bind in practice: the widest populations produce $h \approx 0.02$--$0.05$, well above the constraint.

\subsection{Training Details}

All models use float64 precision, Adam optimiser with piecewise constant LR ($10^{-2}$ for steps 0--2000, $10^{-3}$ for 2000--4000, $10^{-4}$ thereafter), batch size 256, 50 Euler time steps over $T = 1.0$. State stream: 2 hidden layers $\times$ 32 neurons with BatchNorm (momentum 0.01). Law stream: 2 hidden layers $\times$ 32 neurons, no normalisation. Competitive factor network $g$: Linear(embed\_dim $\to$ 16) $\to$ ReLU $\to$ Linear(16 $\to$ 1) $\to$ Sigmoid, with output $0.01 + 0.99 \cdot \text{sigmoid}(\cdot)$ to enforce $h \in (0.01, 1]$. Population shapes are sampled as $q_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_q^2)$ clipped to $[-10, 10]$. Training uses the batch itself as the particle system --- no separate simulation. All code: \url{https://github.com/cgarryZA/DeepMVBSDEJ}.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Representation determines learnability.}
The central finding is that mean-field effects are \emph{present in the model} but \emph{invisible to the solver} unless the law encoder exposes the relevant distributional features. This has implications beyond this specific problem: any deep learning system that conditions on shared population statistics via broadcast features must account for normalisation layer interactions.

\paragraph{Architectural lessons.}
BatchNorm, while critical for Deep BSDE training stability~\cite{han2018solving}, is fundamentally incompatible with broadcast conditioning signals. The two-stream architecture (BN on per-agent features, no BN on shared features) resolves this cleanly. FiLM-style modulation~\cite{perez2018film} would be a natural alternative. More broadly, these findings suggest that any deep learning system conditioning on shared population statistics must carefully consider representation and normalisation, as architectural choices can mask genuine interaction effects.

\paragraph{Two pathways for law dependence.}\label{sec:pathway}
The architecture provides two channels: (i)~$h(\Phi(\mup))$ entering the generator (affecting value via execution rates), and (ii)~$\Phi(\mup)$ entering the subnet directly (affecting $Z$ and thus quotes). We disentangle these by cross-feeding: supplying one population's embedding to $h$ and another's to the subnet (\Cref{tab:disentangle}).

\begin{table}[!htb]
\centering
\caption{Pathway disentanglement: which channel drives which effect?}
\label{tab:disentangle}
\begin{tabular}{@{}llll@{}}
\toprule
Condition & $h$ & $Z^q$ & $\delta_a$ \\
\midrule
Full narrow & 0.404 & $-0.012$ & 0.654 \\
Full wide & 0.053 & $-0.049$ & 0.497 \\
$h$=wide, subnet=narrow & 0.053 & $-0.012$ & \textbf{0.654} \\
$h$=narrow, subnet=wide & 0.404 & $-0.049$ & \textbf{0.497} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The quote shift comes \emph{entirely} from the subnet pathway: swapping the subnet embedding changes $\delta_a$ from $0.654$ to $0.497$ regardless of $h$. Swapping $h$ alone does not move quotes at all. The competitive factor $h(\Phi(\mup))$ learns a meaningful, monotone mapping from population dispersion to execution scaling, but has at most a weak effect on the value level and no observable effect on the learned policy when acting alone. Population-dependent controls arise entirely from direct conditioning of the policy subnet. The two channels are functionally independent in the trained model.

As a control experiment, we train an $h$-only model where the law embedding enters $f$ (via $h$, with properly trained competitive factor network) but \emph{not} the subnet. The competitive factor varies substantially across population shapes ($\approx 7.5\times$) and is correctly ordered: narrow $= 0.412$, medium $= 0.260$, wide $= 0.055$. However, the learned control is completely invariant: $Z^q = 0.0017$ regardless of population shape (identical to four decimal places), quotes are identical across all populations, and $Y_0 = 0.144$ is similar to the full MV model ($0.142$) but not meaningfully different from what the no-coupling baseline achieves through the same training dynamics. While $h$ encodes economically meaningful population dependence, this dependence is not translated into policy variation through the BSDE dynamics when acting alone. Direct subnet conditioning is necessary for policy-level mean-field dependence.

\paragraph{Generalisation across population families.}
The trained model (MomentEncoder, $\phi = 0.1$) generalises to distribution types not seen during training. Evaluating on uniform, bimodal, skewed (chi-squared), and heavy-tailed (Student's $t$, $\nu=3$) populations, the competitive factor $h$ tracks population dispersion monotonically: uniform$[-1,1]$ ($h = 0.34$), bimodal$\pm 2$ ($h = 0.16$), skewed ($h = 0.08$), heavy-tail ($h = 0.12$). Heavy-tailed distributions produce lower $h$ than Gaussians of similar variance, suggesting the model is sensitive to tail behaviour beyond the second moment. This is not memorisation of Gaussian shapes.

\paragraph{Economic interpretation.}
The learned competitive factor $h(\Phi(\mup))$ maps wider inventory distributions to lower fill rates, consistent with the intuition that dispersed populations contain more aggressive competitors. The resulting policy adaptations (narrower ask under competition, wider bid) are economically rational inventory management responses. We emphasise that this is a learned mapping, not a derived equilibrium condition: the economic story is plausible but not uniquely identified from the current evidence. Critically, the policy dependence arises from the subnet's direct use of law features to modulate $Z^q$, not from $h$ affecting the BSDE dynamics. The architecture learns to use population information, but through a more direct channel than the model's intended mean-field mechanism.

% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have shown that deep BSDE solvers require explicit architectural pathways for mean-field information to affect learned controls. Two silent failure modes --- BatchNorm erasure of broadcast features and mean-pooling collapse under symmetric inputs --- can mask the signal entirely. After correcting both, the solver produces population-dependent controls that disappear under placebos and align directionally with finite-difference comparative statics.

However, the most important finding is structural: even when the BSDE generator correctly incorporates law dependence and learns economically sensible competitive factors, this does not translate into policy variation. All observed control dependence arises from direct conditioning of the policy subnet on population features. Learning a correct economic signal is not sufficient for it to shape behaviour through the BSDE dynamics alone.

The deeper lesson is methodological: null results in mean-field deep learning may reflect architectural failures rather than genuine model invariance, and positive results require careful pathway analysis to determine whether the dependence operates through the intended mechanism.

\paragraph{Limitations.} We demonstrate learned sensitivity to population structure, not equilibrium-correct sensitivity: the competitive factor network learns a monotone mapping (wider $\to$ lower $h$) but we have not verified that this corresponds to a Nash equilibrium fixed point or that fictitious play converges. The policy dependence arises from direct subnet conditioning, not from the BSDE generator pathway, so it is not clear that the learned controls correspond to the solution of the underlying mean-field control problem. Multi-seed results (\Cref{tab:multiseed}) confirm the $h$-gap is robust ($0.343 \pm 0.072$), but the $Y_0$ magnitude has higher variance across seeds ($\pm 0.026$). The adverse selection model uses a simple EMA signal. The experiments are at $d = 2$--$3$.

\paragraph{Future work.} Fictitious play convergence verification to establish whether the learned mapping corresponds to a Nash equilibrium; FiLM conditioning (feature-wise linear modulation) as an alternative to direct concatenation that may strengthen the generator pathway; quantitative comparative statics matching against finite-difference benchmarks; DeepSets with variance-sensitive features ($q^2$ pooling); common noise via deep signatures~\cite{hu2025deep}.
